Teaspresso

2017-08-13 4:21 pm

I am an avid coffee drinker. I love a strong cup of joe any time of day, and I really enjoy making espressos, cappuccinos, lattes, etc. Don’t be surprised to see a coffee post on here some time in the future. This post, however, is not about coffee. This whole thing started out as a discussion about coffee on Discord.

We jokingly talked about how coffee is an important part of the day, when someone interjected that they’re sensitive to coffee and have to drink decaf, which is hard to come by if you want quality goods. We came to the conclusion that coffee and tea are basically the same thing; coffee is just “coffee bean tea”. This statement sparked an idea in my mind; if coffee is just tea, does that mean that we can use tea in the same way as we use coffee? For instance, can we make espresso using tea? Obviously it’s possible to put tea in an espresso machine, but how does it turn out? I felt that I had to try, so that’s what this post is about.

First off I needed the actual tea. I have made teaspresso on four kinds of tea: a black tea, a green tea (with some dried fruit) and a rooibos tea. Unfortunately I don’t know more about the teas, beacuse I bought them a while back. The coffee is ground quite finely when making espresso, to help build up pressure in the filter among other things. Because of this I ground the tea with a mortar and pestle somewhat finely. In the image above, the green tea is to the right, the rooibos is in the middle, and the black is to the left. The pulled shots are in the picture below, and they’re in the same order as in the picture of the grounds.

First out is the black tea, which is being pulled in the picture to the right. To my surprise the tea actualy got quite a nice crema. Not in the same way as a good cup of epresso of course, but there was still a crema on the shot. The tea itself didn’t smell a lot (disclaimer: my sense of smell is somewhat reduced, so I might be wrong about this), but it smelled of black tea. The texture of the teaspresso was quite silky, almost in the same way an espresso is. It tasted pleasantly tea-y, with all the sweet notes and none of the bitter notes. It was also somewhat tart with a citrus feeling to it. It did not taste a lot. As an espresso tastes like a really strong cup of coffee (well, not quite, but you get the picture) I expected the same from the tea, but I was wrong. If I would’ve brewed the tea normally and steeped it for say 5-6 minutes it would likely have tasted more than this did. The teaspresso did taste somewhat differently though. In a pleasant way.

I moved on to the rooibos. Same procedure as last time. The color of is shot was amazing, especially while pulling it. A lovely orange hue. The color did tone down a bit once the teaspresso settled, but it was still quite a livid color. Tastewise, this was a completely different beast than the black tea. Firstly, this smelled of rooibos tea a lot. Secondly, this tea really packed a punch. It didn’t taste tea very much, but was instead intensly peppery. It was almost like biting black pepper, but not quite as strong. The texture wasn’t as silky as the black teaspresso, and it had an aftertaste that I could not quite identify. A very different experience, but not an unpleasant one. Maybe not something anyone would like, but I sure did.

Finally there’s the green tea. It had almost no smell (again, I’m not the best judge of this), but it had much taste. Unfortunately, it was not a good taste. The green teaspresso was increadibly bitter, and left a very dry feeling in my mouth. It did have some sweetness hiding behind the bitterness which came forward if you sipped it in very small amounts. A friend on the Discord-channel tried as well and didn’t feel it though. Since he didn’t have any dried fruit in his tea we came to the conclusion that this might be what caused it. All in all this was not very good at all, and I wouldn’t recommend it.

Conclusions then. I think this was quite an exciting experiment, and I’d like to try this with other kinds of tea as well (yerba mate and white tea are next) on the list. We discussed the failure of the green tea on Discord, and theorized that the increased surface area of the ground tea might very well mean that more tannines are released into the water, making it bitter. I’m therefore considering pulling another shot of the green teaspresso without grinding the leaves and see if that turns out better.

I hope you enjoyed this, I sure did! Therefore you might expect to see more stuff like this, both on tea, coffee and perhaps the occational cocktail. Only time will tell. Until next time!
